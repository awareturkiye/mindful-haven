Understanding Mental Health Conditions

Mental health conditions are common, treatable, and nothing to be ashamed of. Understanding them is the first step towards recovery.

😰 Anxiety

Anxiety is a feeling of unease — worry or fear — that can be mild or severe. While some anxiety is normal, anxiety disorders occur when worry becomes persistent and overwhelming.

Anxiety affects around 1 in 6 people in the UK. It is one of the most common mental health conditions and is very treatable.

Feeling nervous or restless · Racing heartbeat · Sweating or trembling · Difficulty concentrating · Sleep problems · Avoiding situations that trigger anxiety · Muscle tension · Stomach upset

Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD) · Panic Disorder · Social Anxiety Disorder · Specific Phobias · Separation Anxiety · Agoraphobia · Health Anxiety

Self-help strategies like breathing exercises, mindfulness, and reducing caffeine can help. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is highly effective. Your GP can also refer you for talking therapies or prescribe medication if needed.

💙 Depression

Depression is more than feeling unhappy or fed up for a few days. It's a persistent low mood that affects your ability to function and enjoy life. Depression is real, common, and treatable.

Around 3 in 100 people in England will experience depression in any given week. You are not alone.

Persistent low mood or sadness · Loss of interest in activities · Fatigue and low energy · Feelings of worthlessness or guilt · Difficulty thinking or concentrating · Changes in sleep or appetite · Thoughts of self-harm or suicide

Major Depressive Disorder · Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia) · Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) · Postnatal Depression · Bipolar Depression · Psychotic Depression

Talking therapies such as CBT or counselling can be very effective. Antidepressant medication may also be recommended. Lifestyle changes — including exercise, social connection, and routine — can support recovery alongside professional treatment.

🌊 Stress

Stress is our body's response to pressure. Some stress can be useful, motivating us to take action. But when stress is chronic or overwhelming, it can harm both mental and physical health.

Headaches or muscle tension · Difficulty sleeping · Irritability and mood swings · Difficulty concentrating · Digestive problems · Feeling overwhelmed · Increased use of alcohol or substances

Identify your triggers · Practise deep breathing and mindfulness · Set clear boundaries · Exercise regularly · Connect with others · Take regular breaks · Talk to a professional if it persists

🌙 Sleep Problems

Sleep and mental health are deeply intertwined. Poor sleep worsens anxiety and depression. Many mental health problems disrupt sleep, creating a difficult cycle that can be unravelled with the right support.

Insomnia (difficulty falling or staying asleep) · Hypersomnia (sleeping too much) · Nightmares and night terrors · Sleep apnoea · Restless legs · Disrupted sleep rhythm

Keep a consistent sleep schedule · Avoid screens 1 hour before bed · Create a dark, cool, quiet room · Limit caffeine after midday · Exercise regularly (not close to bedtime) · Try a calming bedtime routine · Practice relaxation techniques

🔄 OCD

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) involves unwanted, intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviours or mental acts (compulsions) performed to reduce anxiety. It is not about being "neat" — it can be deeply distressing.

Intrusive, unwanted thoughts · Excessive cleaning or checking · Needing things in a specific order · Counting or repeating rituals · Fear of harming yourself or others · Seeking constant reassurance

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) therapy is the most effective treatment for OCD. CBT is also used. In some cases, SSRIs (antidepressants) may be prescribed alongside therapy. Talk to your GP for a referral.

🛡️ PTSD

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. It causes the nervous system to become stuck in a state of danger, long after the event has passed.

Flashbacks and vivid memories · Nightmares · Emotional numbness · Hypervigilance (feeling constantly on guard) · Avoidance of triggers · Irritability and anger outbursts · Difficulty trusting others

Trauma-focused CBT and EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) are recommended primary treatments. Both should be provided by qualified practitioners. Don't wait — PTSD is very treatable with the right specialist support.

Get Support for PTSD
Condition

🍃 Eating Disorders

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions characterised by an unhealthy relationship with food, eating, and body image. They affect people of all ages, genders, and backgrounds.

Anorexia Nervosa (severely restricting food) · Bulimia Nervosa (bingeing and purging) · Binge Eating Disorder · ARFID (avoidant/restrictive food intake) · Warning signs include extreme dietary control, distorted body image, food rituals, and hiding eating habits.

Early intervention improves outcomes significantly. Treatment typically involves a team including therapists, dietitians, and sometimes medical professionals. BEAT (beateatingdisorders.org.uk) provides specialist support and helplines.

⚡ Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder causes extreme mood swings — from emotional highs (mania or hypomania) to severe lows (depression). These episodes can last days, weeks, or months, but with the right support, many people live well with bipolar disorder.

Mania: Feeling unusually happy or irritable · Reduced need for sleep · Racing thoughts · Impulsive decisions. Depression: Low energy · Hopelessness · Loss of interest · Difficulty functioning · Both: Disrupted sleep and appetite, difficulty in relationships.

Mood stabilising medication is often the foundation of treatment. Psychoeducation, CBT, and family therapy also help. Regular routines and sleep are especially important. Work with your GP or psychiatrist to find the right plan.
