Self-Harm

Compassionate, non-judgmental information about self-harm — understanding it, finding alternatives, and taking steps toward recovery.

🌿
A note before you read: This page discusses self-harm in an honest but carefully considered way. We follow safe messaging guidelines — we do not describe specific methods or graphic details. If you are in distress right now, please get help here or call 116 123 (Samaritans, free, 24/7).

🆘 Need help right now?

You don't have to be in immediate danger to reach out. If you're struggling with urges to hurt yourself, please contact one of these free services:

📞 Samaritans — 116 123 💬 Shout — Text 85258 👶 Childline — 0800 1111 🚨 Emergency — 999

All lines are free and confidential. You don't need to be in crisis to call — you can ring just to talk.

Self-Harm Information

You are not alone. Self-harm is more common than many people realise. It doesn't mean you're broken, attention-seeking, or beyond help. It usually means you're in pain and haven't yet found another way to cope. That can change — and you deserve support to find it.

Why people self-harm

Self-harm is often a way of coping with overwhelming emotions that feel impossible to manage or express any other way. People describe it as:

  • A way to make emotional pain feel more tangible or real
  • A method of releasing feelings that feel impossible to express in words
  • A way to feel something when everything feels numb
  • A form of self-punishment driven by shame or guilt
  • A way to regain a sense of control when life feels overwhelming

There is no single reason. Whatever yours is — it's real, it matters, and it can be addressed with the right support.

When you feel the urge — things to try instead

These are not about simply resisting. They are about giving your body and mind an alternative way to process what you're feeling in that moment.

🧊
Hold ice cubes

The intense cold sensation can interrupt the urge and give your body something physical to focus on.

🏃
Move intensely

Run, do star jumps, punch a pillow. Physical exertion can release the emotional pressure quickly.

🎨
Express it creatively

Draw, write, paint or scream into a pillow. Give the emotion somewhere to go outside your body.

📞
Call or text someone

Call a helpline (116 123), text a friend, or message a crisis service. You don't have to be alone with this.

🌬️
Box breathing

Inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat until the intensity begins to drop.

🛁
Intense sensations (safe)

A very cold or warm shower, strong flavours, or smelling something sharp can shift your nervous system state.

💡 Important: These alternatives work differently for different people. If you don't find them helpful, that's okay — it doesn't mean you've failed. It means you need personalised support to find what works for you, which a therapist or counsellor can help with.

Steps to getting help

1

Tell someone you trust

This is often the hardest step. You don't have to explain everything — just saying "I've been hurting myself and I need help" is enough to start.

2

See your GP

Your GP will not judge you. They can assess your welfare, tend to any physical injuries, and refer you to specialist mental health support. Everything is confidential.

3

Ask for a mental health referral

Therapies like DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy) and CBT are very effective for helping people who self-harm find alternative strategies to manage difficult emotions.

4

Be patient and kind with yourself

Recovery is not linear. There may be setbacks. A relapse doesn't erase your progress — it's part of the process. You deserve compassion, from others and from yourself.

Ready to take the next step?

You deserve support. There are people who understand what you're going through.

What you're feeling is valid too. Discovering that someone you care about self-harms can feel shocking, frightening, and overwhelming. You may not know what to say or do. This section is here to help.

✅ Things that can help

✓

Stay calm and listen

Your reaction in the first moment matters enormously. Try not to panic or show disgust — respond with warmth and openness above all else.

✓

Ask open questions

"How long have you been feeling this way?" or "What's been going on for you?" gives them space to share at their own pace.

✓

Believe and validate them

Trust what they share. Say "Thank you for telling me — that must have taken courage." Don't minimise or compare their pain.

✓

Gently encourage professional help

Offer to help them see a GP, accompany them to an appointment, or look into options together. Don't force — support.

✓

Look after yourself too

Supporting someone through this is emotionally demanding. Speak to a professional or support group yourself if you need it.

⚠️ Things to avoid

✗

Don't react with anger or disgust

Even if you feel frightened, a harsh reaction may cause them to shut down and stop seeking help altogether.

✗

Don't dismiss or minimise

Saying "it's just a phase" or "others have it worse" invalidates their pain and damages trust quickly.

✗

Don't make promises you can't keep

If they ask you to keep it secret, say honestly: "I care too much about you to promise that if I think you're in danger."

✗

Don't try to fix it alone

You are not their therapist. Your role is to be present and supportive, and to help connect them with professional care.

📞

Support for concerned family and friends

You can call Samaritans (116 123) yourself if you're struggling to cope with worry about someone. They support those concerned about others, not just those in crisis themselves.

🚨 When to act immediately

If someone has injured themselves severely, is unconscious, or has taken an overdose — call 999 immediately. In this situation, their physical safety comes first. You can stay with them and keep them calm while waiting for help.

What is self-harm?

Self-harm is when someone deliberately hurts themselves as a way of coping with emotional pain, distress, or difficult feelings. It is not usually about wanting to die — it is most often a coping mechanism to manage overwhelming emotions.

Self-harm affects people of all ages, genders, and backgrounds. It is estimated that around 1 in 12 young people in the UK self-harm. It is more common than many people realise, and the shame associated with it often prevents people from seeking help.

Common signs someone may be self-harming

These signs do not confirm self-harm, but may be reasons to gently start a conversation:

Physical signs
  • Unexplained cuts, burns, or bruises
  • Wearing long sleeves or trousers in warm weather
  • Reluctance to show certain areas of the body
  • Spending long periods alone in rooms
Emotional & behavioural signs
  • Extreme mood swings or emotional numbness
  • Social withdrawal from friends and family
  • Expressions of worthlessness or self-loathing
  • Giving away possessions or saying goodbyes

The link between self-harm and mental health

Self-harm often co-occurs with other mental health conditions, including:

Depression Anxiety PTSD Eating Disorders Borderline Personality Disorder Trauma

This means that treating the underlying mental health condition — through therapy, medication, and support — is often central to helping someone stop self-harming. Recovery is absolutely possible with the right support.

Recovery

Treatment & Recovery

Recovery from self-harm is possible. These approaches are used by mental health professionals to help people find lasting change.

🧠

DBT — Dialectical Behaviour Therapy

The most widely recommended therapy for self-harm. DBT teaches emotional regulation, distress tolerance, mindfulness, and interpersonal skills — all the tools self-harm is often used to replace.

🗣️

CBT — Cognitive Behavioural Therapy

Helps identify the thoughts and patterns that lead to self-harm, and builds healthier responses to difficult emotions. Often used alongside DBT or other therapeutic approaches.

👥

Peer Support & Group Therapy

Connecting with others who understand can reduce shame and isolation dramatically. Facilitated support groups for self-harm are available through the NHS and charities like MIND and SANE.
